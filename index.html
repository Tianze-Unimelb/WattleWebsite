Early project prototype for the Wattle Fellowship

Everyday sustainability, made easier for international students.

A peer-led guide and workshop idea to help international students in Melbourne make low-waste, low-carbon choices feel practical, culturally accessible and less intimidating.

View the prototype Download the one-page PDF

What this shows

  • Lived experience: moving through sustainability in a new city.
  • Action seed: a clear, testable peer-led project idea.
  • Wattle mindset: start small, listen, test, adapt.

Why this matters

The problem is not lack of care. It is lack of context, confidence and accessible support.

International students often arrive in Melbourne while learning many systems at once: supermarkets, shared kitchens, rental routines, public transport, budgeting, language and cultural expectations.

Sustainability advice can feel abstract or judgmental when students are still trying to belong. This prototype starts from a simple question: how can everyday sustainable choices become easier to understand, easier to practise and easier to sustain?

Three everyday starting points

Small choices that can become shared student habits.

These are not the whole of sustainability. They are practical entry points where peer support can turn concern into action.

Food waste

Simple shopping habits, shared meals, leftovers, low-cost cooking and fridge routines.

  • Plan flexible meals before shopping
  • Share bulk items with housemates
  • Create a “use first” fridge habit

Transport

Myki confidence, public transport planning, walking, cycling and low-carbon everyday movement.

  • Make public transport feel less confusing
  • Plan low-carbon trips around daily routines
  • Support walking and cycling confidence

Consumption

Second-hand choices, borrowing, sharing and buying less while living on a student budget.

  • Find second-hand and borrowing options
  • Reduce unnecessary first-semester purchases
  • Make low-cost student living more sustainable

Prototype

A guide and workshop that students could actually use.

This is designed as a first test, not a finished solution. The goal is to learn from students and improve the resource through feedback.

Example resource

5-step food waste starter guide

  1. Plan two simple meals before shopping.
  2. Buy ingredients that work across more than one dish.
  3. Share bulk items with housemates or friends.
  4. Keep a “use first” area in the fridge.
  5. Turn leftovers into one flexible meal each week.

How I would test it through Wattle

Start small, listen carefully, improve with peers.

1

Listen

Speak with international students about everyday barriers, assumptions and needs.

2

Prototype

Create a simple digital guide with practical tips and culturally accessible examples.

3

Pilot

Run one peer-led workshop and observe what students find useful or unclear.

4

Improve

Collect feedback, adapt the resource and identify what could grow next.

What success would look like

Students feel more confident, less overwhelmed and more able to take one practical sustainable step in their everyday life.
